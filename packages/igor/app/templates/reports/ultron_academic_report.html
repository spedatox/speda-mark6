Retrieval Depth and Factual Grounding in Long-Context Summarisation

Ultron · Research Division, SPEDA Mark VI
ULT-RES-2026-019 · 21 July 2026 · 480 instances · three corpora
Abstract
Long-context models are routinely asked to summarise material that exceeds what a single retrieval pass can surface. This paper asks whether deeper retrieval improves factual grounding, or merely enlarges the surface on which unsupported claims can form. We evaluate four configurations — no retrieval, shallow, deep, and iterative retrieval with verification — across 480 summarisation instances drawn from three corpora of technical documents, holding the generator, prompt, and decoding parameters fixed. Grounding is measured as sentence-level attribution to a retrieved span, scored by two automated judges and a human-adjudicated subsample. Grounding improves monotonically from no retrieval to deep retrieval, with the largest single gain falling between the shallow and deep configurations, and then plateaus. Iterative retrieval with verification yields no significant gain over deep retrieval while tripling token cost. Unsupported claims concentrate in synthesis sentences rather than extractive ones, which locates the failure in the generation step rather than in retrieval coverage. We conclude that retrieval depth is subject to sharp diminishing returns, and that verification effort is better spent on synthesis-heavy passages than applied uniformly.
Keywords retrieval-augmented generation · factual grounding · long-context summarisation · attribution · evaluation methodology

1Introduction

Summarisation over collections that exceed a model's effective attention span is now a production workload rather than a research curiosity.[1] The standard mitigation is retrieval augmentation: select source spans, place them in context, generate. The implicit premise is that grounding failures come from absent evidence, and so that retrieving more evidence removes them.

That premise is seldom tested on its own. Existing evaluations report end-task scores that fold fluency, coverage, and grounding into one number.[2,3] We isolate grounding and vary only retrieval depth.

1.1Contributions

Three. A controlled comparison of four retrieval configurations against a fixed generator; a sentence-level attribution protocol with reported inter-judge agreement; and an error analysis partitioning unsupported claims by sentence type, which localises the failure to synthesis rather than to retrieval.

2Related work

Retrieval augmentation was formalised early and has since split into sparse, dense, and hybrid families.[4] Attribution research proceeded largely in parallel, producing span-level annotation schemes that we adopt with minor modification.[5,6] The nearest prior study varies retrieval breadth rather than depth and reports no agreement statistics, so its effect sizes cannot be compared with ours directly.[7]

3Method

3.1Configurations

All four share one generator and one prompt, differing only in the number of retrieved spans placed in context and whether a verification pass runs. Depth k denotes retrieved spans.

3.2Grounding score

For a summary S of sentences si and retrieved set R, grounding is the proportion of sentences attributable to at least one span:

G(S,R) = |S|−1 Σi 1[ ∃r ∈ R : attr(si,r) ] (1)

where attr is the adjudicated attribution judgement. Pure discourse markers carry no verifiable content and are excluded from the denominator.

3.3Corpora

Instances were drawn from three corpora of technical documents, stratified so no corpus contributes more than 40 %. Document length ranges from 4 000 to 61 000 tokens, median 18 400.

4.1Where the failures sit

Partitioning unsupported claims by sentence type, 78 % fall in synthesis sentences — those combining two or more retrieved spans — although such sentences are only 31 % of the corpus. Extractive sentences fail at 3.1 %, which is the annotation noise floor.[6]

5Discussion

The plateau between the deep and iterative configurations is the consequential result. Were grounding failures caused by missing evidence, a verification pass that triggers further retrieval would recover them. It does not. The error analysis supplies a mechanism: failures appear where the generator bridges two spans with an inferential step that neither span supports.

Practical reading Buy depth once, then stop. Spend the saved budget on verifying synthesis-heavy passages rather than on verifying everything at uniform cost.

5.1Limitations

Three bound the conclusions. The generator is fixed, so transfer across model families is untested. All corpora are English technical prose. Automated judgement agreed with human adjudication on 91.4 % of a 600-sentence subsample (κ = 0.82), leaving measurement error that is small against the reported effects but not zero.

6Conclusion

Retrieval depth improves grounding with sharply diminishing returns past a moderate depth, and uniform verification buys little at considerable cost. Effort belongs at the synthesis step, where unsupported claims concentrate. Targeted verification conditioned on sentence type is the obvious next experiment.
